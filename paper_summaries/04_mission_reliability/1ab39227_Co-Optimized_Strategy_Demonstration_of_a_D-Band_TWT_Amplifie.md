## Abstract
D-band is a key enabler for next-generation high-speed wireless links. To fully realize its potential, efficient and stable power sources are crucial. This article presents a D-band traveling wave tube (TWT) amplifier, designed to meet the requirements of wireless communication systems and with potential for large-scale production. The device is realized through a co-optimized strategy, including optimizations of the electron optical system (EOS), RF circuit, and assembly process, demonstrating a pathway for reproducible fabrication. The Pierce electron gun features a cylindrical focusing electrode, which provides better operability and enhanced alignment tolerance during assembly. When combined with a periodic permanent magnet (PPM) focusing system, the EOS attains a 99.5% beam transmission rate in a short tube test. The slow wave structure (SWS) utilizes a folded waveguide (FWG) unit, characterized by a well-balanced high interaction impedance and a broad bandwidth. A symmetric circuit with identical input and output sections is designed to compensate for the initial launching loss and maintain high gain. Furthermore, by reducing component variety, this configuration could simplify fabrication and increase yield. It is an important advantage for mass production. Well-matched RF windows and step-shaped attenuators are integrated into the circuit to ensure efficient power transmission and circuit stability. Moreover, a stepwise welding and assembly process is employed to reduce the thermal deformation of precision components, thereby enhancing the reliability and success rate of the prototype. In the final hot-test result, the proposed TWT achieves an output power of 10.5–26.5 W within a 10-GHz operating bandwidth (151–161 GHz), with a maximum gain reaching 55.9 dB. The corresponding input drive stays below 1 mW, fulfilling the typical system requirements for communication applications. The development of the final product could provide practical experience in designing and fabricating power sources in the D-band and higher terahertz (THz) frequency ranges.
