## Abstract
Due to limited spectrum availability, both ultrareliable low-latency communication (URLLC), also known as mission-critical, and enhanced mobile broadband (eMBB) applications coexist on the same carrier to enhance spectrum efficiency. Mission-critical applications demand reliable and timely delivery of traffic because if the traffic arrives too late, it is of limited use and may cause degradation in service, economic loss, or even loss of life and property. Conversely, eMBB applications require high throughput. These conflicting requirements create a significant tradeoff between the latency and reliability needs of URLLC and the throughput demands of eMBB applications. This study presents an in-depth, state-of-the-art systematic review of current scheduling schemes for URLLC and eMBB, with a focus on their suitability for the joint scheduling of MC-IoT healthcare and eMBB applications. To ensure replicability and clearly report the study’s main findings, the review is based on Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) guidelines. The survey considered a total of 99 papers published between 2015 and April 2025. Three research questions (RQs) are addressed in this study: 1) what scheduling schemes are currently used for MC-IoT and eMBB applications, and how do they manage spectrum sharing; 2) what challenges and limitations exist in scheduling MC-IoT healthcare and eMBB applications; and 3) what queuing models have been proposed for URLLC and eMBB scheduling, and how well do they meet the performance requirements of MC-IoT healthcare applications. The findings show that although existing reviews on scheduling systems for URLLC and eMBB consider multiple factors for their efficiency, other critical factors, such as traffic burstiness and URLLC traffic differentiation, have not been considered. To the best of our knowledge, this survey is the first to examine existing scheduling schemes for URLLC and eMBB applications while considering critical factors such as traffic burstiness and URLLC traffic differentiation.
