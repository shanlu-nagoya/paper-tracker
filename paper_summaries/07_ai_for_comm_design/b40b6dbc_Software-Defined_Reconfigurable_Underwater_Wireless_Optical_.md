## Abstract
Underwater wireless optical communication (UWOC) systems face an important trade-off between transmission rate and link robustness, which is further exacerbated by channel attenuation and the nonlinearity of opto-electronic conversion. To address these challenges, we propose a software-defined reconfigurable UWOC (SDR-UWOC) system based on laser diode (LD) arrays for optical superimposition without complex electrical digital-to-analog converters (DACs). The system employs a unified hardware platform that supports dynamic modulation and data rate switching between power-superimposed on-off keying (OOK) for robust communication and signal-superimposed pulse amplitude modulation with four levels (PAM4) for high-speed transmission. To mitigate optical device nonlinearities and channel impairments, we develop a hybrid convolutional neural network and long short-term memory (CNN-LSTM) demodulation scheme that combines spatial feature extraction with temporal sequence modeling. Experimental validation over a 1-meter water channel with controlled attenuation demonstrates that OOK superimposition improves attenuation tolerance by 3.1 dB compared to single-LD operation. In PAM4 mode, the optical combination of three LDs extends the transmitter's linear operating region by approximately 2.4-fold and the CNN-LSTM demodulation algorithm achieves a 0.8 dB improvement in attenuation tolerance compared to fixed-threshold decisions. Through software-defined reconfiguration between each modulation mode, the system achieves a 6.9 dB improvement in attenuation tolerance and extends the usable transmission rate range seven-fold (100-700 Mbps) compared to fixed-modulation baselines. This work provides a feasible solution for dynamically balancing data rate and link robustness in complex underwater optical communication networks, showing promising improvements in both link performance and transmission rate flexibility.
