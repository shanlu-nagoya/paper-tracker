## Abstract
Integrated sensing and communication over optical fiber (ISACoF) is a promising route to upgrade widely deployed fiber infrastructures from passive transmission media to self-aware multifunctional platforms. Yet ISACoF design requirements differ markedly across network layers, from long-haul and metro/data-center-interconnect (DCI) links to short-reach access networks and radio access networks (RANs), and a unified systemlevel view remains lacking. This paper presents a cross-layer perspective on ISACoF and reviews our experimentally validated architectures for diverse transmission scenarios. For long-haul and metro/DCI networks, we address sensing-induced nonlinear crosstalk, integration of forward- and backscattering-based sensing, and endogenous sensing enabled by communication channels. We examine dense wavelength-division multiplexing coexistence of distributed acoustic sensing (DAS) and coherent communication, forward-polarization-sensing-triggered area-focused DAS, and optical-supervisory-channel-based sensing using live data streams. For shortreach access networks and RANs, we address sensing trace aliasing, use of legacy infrastructure, and sensing in fiber–wireless converged systems. We review dualcomb-enabled multipath sensing in passive optical networks (PONs), Fresnelreflection-assisted branch monitoring, seamless sensing in radio-over-fiber links, orthogonal frequency-division multiplexing fronthaul with embedded linear frequencymodulated pilots for large-dynamic-range vibration detection, and fiber–wireless collaborative sensing. Organized within a communication-centric framework, these studies reveal tradeoffs among reach, spatial resolution, sensitivity, complexity, and communication penalty, and demonstrate the potential of ISACoF for intelligent, lowcost, multifunctional optical networks.
