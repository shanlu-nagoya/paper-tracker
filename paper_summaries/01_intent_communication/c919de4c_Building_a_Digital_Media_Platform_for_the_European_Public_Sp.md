## Abstract
Introduction
The currently dominant digital platforms are associated with many negative effects on democratic societies, such as disinformation, hate speech, and propaganda. In the case of X, the platform has become a personal megaphone for its owner Elon Musk. The “Great Sysop” has gained considerable power over public opinion formation and is promoting “platform illiberalism” (Magalhães et al.). However, Twitter has also added valuable features that have enhanced public communication, particulary in journalism, which is the focus of the present article. Journalists used Twitter for purposes such as live reporting, news aggregation, including citizens as sources, organising debates (Artwick), and fact checking (Coddington et al.). Hermida coined the term “ambient journalism” to describe journalism’s role on Twitter as a novel “awareness system”.
In 2014, a newsroom survey in Germany compared the use of different platforms in journalism (Neuberger et al.). Twitter was most often used for brief breaking news and real-time reporting. Editorial staff also utilised Twitter and Facebook relatively often for investigation and monitoring. Twitter was used especially to contact elite sources, such as experts and prominent people, and for monitoring their public appearances. Twitter and Facebook were the preferred platforms for user participation in journalism.
Twitter served as a testing ground for new forms of journalism. But this is all history. These achievements have been lost since Elon Musk took over the platform. This raises the questions: how should a common good-oriented platform be designed and governed? What lessons can be learned from the journalistic adaptation of Twitter? I will address these questions by focussing on the transnational European public sphere.
European Public Sphere as Goal
The philosopher Jürgen Habermas (Für Europa), who passed away in March 2026, continued to advocate for European autonomy, integration, and democratisation, particularly in the context of today's polycrisis. He believed that establishing a common European public sphere by transnationalising the many national public spheres is essential (Habermas, Demokratie 188–91). But the European public sphere still has significant shortcomings (e.g., Brüggemann and Kleinen-von Königslöw; Brüggemann et al.). There is a lack of European media, audiences interested in European issues, and European perspectives in national media. Europeanisation is particularly evident in the vertical dimension (“looking towards Brussels”), but less so in the horizontal dimension. Looking beyond national borders at other national discourses is therefore crucial here. Another weakness is the low citizen participation in the European public sphere.
The question of a European public sphere has regained importance in recent years. In light of the current geopolitical situation, the European Union (EU) is striving for greater digital sovereignty. This concept emphasises state authority over the Internet, replacing the earlier concept of “multistakeholderism”, which viewed the Internet as a distinct, decentralised, and transnational space beyond the reach of politics (Pohle and Santianello). The call for digital sovereignty encompasses not only the technical and economic dimension, but also the political and cultural dimension. Digital sovereignty “must not be conflated with isolation or protectionism, in the European context, it can be described as the capacity of being independent while remaining open to collaboration and committed to shared values such as transparency, democracy, and the rule of law” (Bria et al. 10). European values are often cited as the goal of digital sovereignty (e.g., Di Marco et al.; Kagermann and Wilhelm; Roberts et al.), but are not precisely defined.
The EU is often criticised for over-regulating platforms and AI, hindering innovation and competitiveness. However, there are serious doubts as to whether this criticism is valid (Bradford, False Choice). Nevertheless, the EU must not only regulate, but also provide better platforms. This requires a change in mindset: regulation only defines the minimum normative requirements that must be met in every case. This negative approach must then be complemented by a positive one that describes how platforms can promote the common good. The EU could offer a more appealing alternative to the libertarian approach of the US and the authoritarian approach of China (Bradford, Digital Empires).
Public Sphere and Public Value
But how can the common good be defined? In the media sector, it is usually referred to as “public value”. This management concept was introduced by Mark H. Moore in his seminal book Creating Public Value. It was intended as a practical framework for public sector management, offering an alternative to the market-oriented, neo-liberal “new public management” and its premise that the public good is simply the sum of individual consumer interests. In contrast to this market-oriented view, the public value concept focusses on contributions to society’s common good beyond individual self-interest (Alford and Hughes 132; Moore, “Reflections” 366–9).
The BBC adopted it in its programmatic statement Building Public Value. In this statement, public value was defined through three “public principles” (universality, fairness and equity, accountability) and five “public purposes” (democratic value, cultural and creative value, educational value, social and community value, global value) (7–8). A recent paper, Creating and Measuring Dynamic Public Value at the BBC, distinguishes between individual value, social value, and industrial value (Mazzucato et al. 23–8). The BBC’s public value test for public broadcasting outlets has been adopted by many EU countries (Collins; Donders).
Some scholars have criticised a lack of clarity about what public value means substantially (e.g., Benington 42–6; Collins 175). Others have argued that public value cannot be defined “in some absolute sense” (Alford and Hughes 133) because public value must be, at least partially, open to the outcomes of democratic political processes (Alford and O’Flynn 175–8; Benington 233–4). The concept of hybrid common good offers a solution for these contradictory expectations (Blum; Neuberger, Auftrag). According to this approach, the common good comprises a substantive component consisting of liberal-democratic values to be realised in the value creation process, alongside public discourse as procedural component, in which those values are interpreted and concretised. These values are the “guardrails”, setting the boundaries for possible outcomes of the democratic public discourse, and also the standards for the discourse itself.
How can the European values be defined more precisely? One obvious option is to derive them from normative theories of democracy. Several proposals have been made as to how normative theories of democracy can be applied to the public sphere and journalism (e.g., Christians et al. 95–105, 125–7; Ferree et al.; Gerhards; Martinsen; McQuail; Strömbäck). While these typologies differ, the theories agree that the media should at least create a common public sphere and a shared body of knowledge on current topics of public interest. Both are necessary prerequisites for the formation of individual and collective opinion. Valid and shared knowledge requires news production to be based on journalistic standards and to allow freedom of information and equal access to knowledge. However, beyond this, the theories make different demands regarding the process of opinion formation. Deliberative theory sets higher normative expectations for discourse progression than liberal and participatory theory (Martinsen). It demands a rational, respectful, and domination-free discourse in which the persuasiveness of the better argument—in the best case—leads to consensus. In summary, nine values of the public sphere can be derived from theories of democracy, whose normative expectations partially overlap: news and discourse quality, freedom, equality, diversity, power symmetry, criticism and control, security, and integration (Neuberger, Öffentlichkeit).
Initiatives to Build a European Media Platform
A more democratic digital public sphere cannot be achieved simply by regulating existing platform and AI companies. It is equally important to create media platforms and AI applications that are oriented towards the common good as an alternative to the commercial offerings that currently dominate the market. Although Habermas acknowledged the potential of the digital public sphere to contribute to the emancipation of citizens, he viewed the Internet as an “ambivalent explosive force” (Habermas, New Structural 35). He missed a “substitute for the professional selection and discursive examination of contents based on accepted cognitive standards” (38–39) on these platforms.
Since 2017, there has been an ongoing discussion about the idea of a European media platform oriented towards the common good that would fulfil European values, create a European public sphere, and strengthen Europe’s digital sovereignty. Ulrich Wilhelm, the director of the Bavarian Broadcasting Corporation (Bayerischer Rundfunk), was the first and strongest advocate of this idea. He expressed concern that “Europe is in danger of losing digital sovereignty over its defining values” (Wilhelm 15; translated by the author).
