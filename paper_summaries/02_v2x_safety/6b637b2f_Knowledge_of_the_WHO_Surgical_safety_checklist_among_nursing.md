# Knowledge of the WHO Surgical safety checklist among nursing students in a tertiary care hospital: A pre – post interventional study

## Abstract
Background
The World Health Organization (WHO) Surgical Safety Checklist (SSC) is an important patient safety tool
designed to improve communication, teamwork, and compliance with essential perioperative safety practices.
Early introduction of patient safety concepts during nursing education may enhance future implementation of safe
surgical practices. However, knowledge regarding the Surgical Safety Checklist among nursing students remains
limited.
Objectives
To assess the knowledge of the WHO Surgical Safety Checklist among nursing students and evaluate the
effectiveness of an educational intervention on knowledge acquisition and retention.
Materials and Methods
A pre–post interventional study was conducted among nursing students in a tertiary care teaching hospital over a
period of six months. The sample size was calculated using G*Power software with an effect size of 0.15, alpha
error of 0.05, power of 90%, and repeated measurements. After accounting for a 10% non-response rate, the
required sample size was estimated to be 178 and rounded up to 180 participants. A structured questionnaire was
administered before the educational intervention and at four post-intervention intervals: immediately after the
lecture, one month, three months, and six months following the intervention. Data were analysed using SPSS
version 29. Descriptive statistics were expressed as mean ± standard deviation, and Repeated Measures ANOVA
was used to compare knowledge scores across different assessment periods.
Results
A total of 163 nursing students completed the study. The mean age of participants was 20.67 ± 2.47 years, and
females constituted 62.6% of the study population. The mean pre-test knowledge score was 5.41 ± 3.81. Following
the educational intervention, the mean score increased significantly to 16.45 ± 6.24 in the immediate post-test
assessment. Although knowledge scores gradually declined during follow-up, they remained higher than baseline
values at one month (11.64 ± 5.74), three months (10.69 ± 5.02), and six months (7.25 ± 3.23). The improvement
in knowledge scores at all post-intervention assessments was statistically significant (p < 0.001).
Conclusion
The educational intervention significantly improved nursing students’ knowledge regarding the WHO Surgical
Safety Checklist. However, a gradual decline in knowledge retention was observed over time, indicating the need
for periodic reinforcement and continuous patient safety education. Incorporation of recurring training sessions
and simulation-based learning into undergraduate nursing curricula may improve long-term retention and promote
safer surgical practices.
